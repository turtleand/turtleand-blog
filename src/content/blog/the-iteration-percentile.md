## The Email That Almost Went Out

Last month I rewrote a client proposal three times. The first draft was fine. It covered the scope, the timeline, the price. It would have worked.

But something felt off. The structure buried the key insight two paragraphs deep. So I moved it up, tightened the opening, cut a section that repeated itself. That was draft two.

Draft three came after I walked away for ten minutes and came back with fresh eyes. I noticed the tone shifted halfway through, from conversational to formal. Fixed that. Trimmed another 15%. Done.

The whole revision cycle took maybe twenty minutes. And the version that went out was noticeably better than the one I almost sent. Not perfect. Just clearer, tighter, more honest about what mattered.

That gap between "fine" and "noticeably better" has been on my mind lately.

## The Math Behind Iteration

Here's an observation I keep coming back to. Most people do something once and call it done. Write the email, ship the slide deck, publish the draft. If we think of that as the 50th percentile, just doing the thing at all, then each round of revision puts you ahead of roughly half the people still above you.

One revision: 75th percentile. Two: about 87th. Three: 93rd.

The math is rough, obviously. It's a mental model, not a precise formula. But the pattern holds in practice. At each stage, a big chunk of people stop. They had the same ability you did. They just didn't go back and look again.

This isn't about talent or some rare discipline. It's about the simple act of returning to something you already made and asking "what could be better here?"

## Why Most People Stop

For a long time, stopping early made sense. Iteration cost real time and energy. If you're hand-drafting a report, every revision means retyping pages. If you're designing without version control, every change risks breaking what worked. The cost of going back was high enough that "good enough" was a rational stopping point.

And honestly, it still is sometimes. Not everything deserves three passes. A grocery list doesn't need revision. A quick Slack message to a teammate can just be sent.

But for work that matters to you, the calculus has changed.

## What Changed

AI made iteration cheap. Not free, exactly, but close enough that the old cost barrier barely applies anymore.

I can ask a model to review my draft for clarity, and it comes back in seconds with observations I might have caught on pass four or five. I can generate three different structures for the same argument and compare them side by side. I can get a sanity check on tone, on flow, on whether the opening actually earns the reader's attention.

Each of those used to require either a patient colleague or hours of self-editing. Now they take minutes.

The [Quality Is Iterations](https://handbook.turtleand.com) principle from Turtleand's handbook captures this well. Quality isn't a trait some people have and others don't. It's a process. And the process just got dramatically more accessible.

## The Part That's Still Yours

Here's what I find interesting about this. The iterations are cheaper, but the decision to iterate is still entirely human. The model doesn't know if your proposal matters enough to revise. It doesn't know if this blog post is a throwaway thought or something you want to stand behind. You do.

That judgment, knowing when to push further and when to let something be good enough, stays with you. AI handles the mechanics of revision faster. But the taste, the sense of "this isn't quite right yet," that's yours.

And it's one of the clearest places where human input still changes the outcome. You don't need to be the fastest writer or the most technically skilled designer. You just need to be willing to look at your own work one more time and ask an honest question about it.

## A Quiet Advantage

I want to be careful not to oversell this. Iterating more doesn't guarantee great work. Sometimes you revise something five times and it's still mediocre. Sometimes the first draft is the best one and every change makes it worse. Judgment matters more than volume.

But as a general pattern, the willingness to do one more pass is surprisingly rare. Most people stop at "done." If you stop at "done, and I looked at it again," you've quietly moved ahead of a large portion of the field. Not because you're better. Because you came back.

It's available. It's under your control. And in a world where AI handles so much of the heavy lifting, choosing to iterate might be one of the simplest ways to add something distinctly yours to the work.

That's about it. No grand conclusion. Just an observation worth sitting with.
